this movie made me cry non-stop. my first movie from kore-eda and it is the perfect story of a poor japanese family and how they adopt a girl who has been abused by her parents.

every person in the household is rough, but full of love. you can see the subtle but meaningful ways they show each other how they truly care. it is a perfect show case of how asian families say 'i love you' without saying it. 

every adult in the movie has a vice and they are all aware of their shortcomings. yet they are not ignoring it or avoiding it, they have almost accepted their imperfections. this shows them as mature characters and makes their connections more meaningful.

when they can barely afford croquettes and they find a crying alone girl and take her in for the evening, when grandma tends to her wounds from the abuse, when they do not get angry when she wets the bed, it is just a string of unconditional understanding and love. and the fact that they are financially poor, shows how usually the ones with nothing are the ones with biggest hearts.

maybe i've been really emotional today but i couldn't even finish the movie yet, i'm halfway through and i needed to take a break from all the small expressions and mannerisms that kept showing their care for each other. when the mom shows the burn scar she has to Yuri and they have a matching burn scar, and when Yuri is scared that they would beat her up for getting new clothes, these are just so difficult to watch.

movie itself has just been insanely good so far and everything is beautiful.

second half of the movie was just as devastatingly beautiful as the first half. 

i believe this movie has been making me cry a lot because it is continously displaying the connections that families have with each other. even in the most simple movies, it does feel emotional to see a touching family moment. Like in kung fu panda or elemental or really anything. shoplifters has an emotional family connection in almost every scene. 

as the grandma passes away, and the family is starting to crack, it doesn't actually tear them apart emotionally, it shows that their bond it not about blood but about how they care for each other. 

does grandma really not care for Aki, and only kept her for money? while police makes that suggestion, i think audience deep down knows the answer. did the family really try to run away from shota? or rather they didn't want to lose what remains and later reconnect with him, they know that state will take good care of him, but it won't give the emotional connection of a family. shota also shows that he knows this at the end, when he calls osamu "dad". 

and yes, they are broken people, it is said they killed nobuyo's husband in self defense, but it really doesn't feel like that was the case. and they stole shota and lin from their parents, because they cannot bear children themselves. but also they worked hard at their jobs, trying to feed the kids before themselves. they taught shota and lin how to care for others. 

as a society, we villianify people who went through difficulty. did the family really do anything truly wrong here? Yes they may have been bad people in the past, but would anyone truly think Lin was better going back to her previous family? is shoplifting really that bad, when the only suffering part is a huge corporation who is making millions of dollars of low wage workers?

i dont think the movie is trying to make any specific points, other than showing that there is life beyond our simple always chasing success day to day situation. and we get less and less patient, as we get more and more "successful" in a societal sense. 

it was a lot of fun watching the movie, but i'll definitely take a break from kore-eda movies for now :D
